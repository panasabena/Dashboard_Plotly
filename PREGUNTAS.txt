1. Identify your main stakeholders

In Billing Operations, your "internal clients" might be:

Finance / Revenue Assurance → They want to minimize revenue loss and billing errors.
Customer Care / Call Center → They want fewer complaints due to invoice errors.
Marketing & Retention → They are interested in understanding why customers cancel (churn) and if billing is a factor.
IT / Process Owners → They look for efficiencies in billing and collection processes.

2. Ask and observe

What reports do they look at daily or weekly?
What indicators do they check to measure if the billing run was successful?
What are the most frequent billing errors?
In which cases do they have to manually "investigate" customer accounts?

3. Review the data you already have

Even if you don't have queries yet, you can start thinking about use cases. Examples:
% of invoices with adjustments or credits → shows previous billing errors.
Average time between invoice issuance and payment → affects cash flow.
Customers with recurring unpaid invoices → churn risk.
Comparison between what should be billed vs. what was actually billed (revenue leakage).

4. Look for telecommunications benchmarks

In U.S. telcos, some typical billing KPIs are:

First Bill Accuracy (FBA): % of customers whose first bill is correct.

Billing Error Rate: Number of complaints for billing errors out of the total number of invoices.

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): Average number of days to collect on an invoice.

Disputes per 1,000 invoices: Billing disputes by volume.

5. Suggest "quick to implement" ideas

When you build your dashboard or proposals, think of quick wins that are tangible for your bosses, such as:
A dashboard showing the invoices most likely to be disputed.

A heatmap of "zones" or customer segments with the most billing issues.

A comparison between what was billed and what was expected according to the contracted plan (potential revenue leaks).

Delinquency trends across different services (telephony, internet, cable).
